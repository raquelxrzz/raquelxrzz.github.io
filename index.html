One City, Many Clocks: How Different Crimes Follow Distinct Time and Space Patterns in San Francisco

Using police records from San Francisco between 2003 and early 2026, this story looks at how crime changes across time and space. It focuses on how different types of crime follow different daily rhythms and appear in different parts of the city.

When we think about crime in a city, we often imagine it as something static: a number that goes up or down, or a problem concentrated in certain areas.

But crime is not only about where it happens. It is also about when, and how these two dimensions interact.

Instead of focusing on totals, this story looks at patterns: how crime changes over the course of the day, where it concentrates across the city, and what appears when we consider both together.

To explore this, we use different visualizations. First, we look at how crime varies over time. Then, we examine where different crimes are concentrated across San Francisco. Finally, we bring both together to see how each crime type follows its own characteristic rhythm.

The data behind the story

The analysis is based on police records from San Francisco covering the period from 2003 to early 2026. Each record includes information about when an incident occurred, where it took place, and how it was classified.

To keep the story focused, we look at three types of crime: larceny theft, fraud, and prostitution.

These three crime types were chosen because they behave quite differently. Larceny theft tends to follow everyday activity in the city, prostitution is more concentrated at specific times and locations, and fraud is slightly different, since the recorded time often reflects when the incident was reported rather than when it actually happened.

Before the analysis, the data was cleaned to remove incomplete or inconsistent records and to ensure that all observations correspond to locations within San Francisco.

Since the data for 2026 is incomplete, patterns for that year should be interpreted with caution.

How crime changes throughout the day

We start by looking at time. If different types of crime behave differently, the first question is whether they also follow the same daily rhythm.

These crime types do not occur at the same frequency. Larceny theft accounts for most incidents in the dataset, while fraud and prostitution are much less common. For this reason, the figure compares how incidents are distributed over the day, rather than raw counts.

The visualization below shows how incidents are distributed across the 24 hours of the day for each crime type, and how these patterns change over time.

Figure 1. Hourly distribution of larceny theft, fraud, and prostitution across different years. The values are normalized within each crime type, so the figure shows when incidents tend to occur rather than how many are recorded.

The differences between crime types are quite clear. Larceny theft is spread across the day, with a peak in the late afternoon, which suggests that it follows everyday activity in the city. In contrast, prostitution is much more concentrated, with most incidents happening at night and peaking around midnight.

Fraud looks very different. It shows a strong peak at midnight, which is unlikely to reflect when the crime actually happens. Instead, it probably reflects when the incident is reported. This is important to keep in mind, because it shows that the data does not always represent real-world behavior directly.

Overall, different crimes follow very different daily rhythms. Some are linked to everyday routines, others are concentrated at specific times, and some are influenced by how the data is recorded.

The differences between crime types are also clear in space. Prostitution is very concentrated, with most incidents happening in just a few specific areas. This makes it quite different from larceny theft, which appears across many parts of the city and follows a much more spread-out pattern.

Fraud sits somewhere in between. It is less concentrated than prostitution, but still shows some clustering in specific areas. As before, this pattern is not always easy to interpret, since the recorded location may reflect where the incident is processed rather than where it actually happened.

It is also important to remember that volume is not the focus here. Larceny theft appears much more often than the other crimes, but that does not mean it is more concentrated. By looking at each map separately, we can focus on the shape of the distribution rather than the number of incidents.

Another interesting detail is that fraud and larceny theft tend to appear in similar central areas, while prostitution is concentrated in different locations. This suggests that different crimes are linked to different parts of the city and different types of activity.

Overall, just like in time, crime does not follow a single spatial pattern. Some crimes are very localized, others are spread across the city, and each one behaves in a different way.

What happens when time and space are combined

Looking at time and space separately already reveals important differences, but combining them gives a much clearer picture.

Crime does not happen only at a certain time, or only in a certain place. Instead, activity changes across the city over the course of the day, as some areas become more active while others become less important.

Bringing both dimensions together makes the differences even clearer. Larceny theft remains spread across the city throughout the day, increasing during busier hours. Prostitution follows a much more specific pattern, becoming more visible at night and staying concentrated in a small number of locations.

Fraud again stands apart. Its strong peak around midnight is unlikely to reflect real behavior and instead highlights how reporting practices can shape the data.

Overall, some crimes follow the general rhythm of the city, others are tied to very specific places and times, and some are shaped by how the data itself is collected.

Conclusion: One city, different patterns

This analysis started with a simple question: how does crime actually unfold across time and space in San Francisco?

Looking at time shows that different crimes follow different daily rhythms. Looking at space shows that they appear in different parts of the city. But it is only when we look at both together that these differences really become clear.

Each crime type behaves in its own way. Larceny theft is spread across the city and increases with overall activity. Prostitution is much more concentrated, both in time and location. Fraud, on the other hand, behaves differently again, reminding us that the data does not always reflect real-world behaviour directly.

What this shows is that crime is not random. It is connected to how people move through the city, how different areas are used, and sometimes to how incidents are recorded.

In the end, the main idea is quite simple. A city does not follow a single rhythm. Different things happen at different times and in different places, and crime reflects that complexity.

To understand these patterns properly, it is important to look at both when and where, while also being aware of what the data can and cannot tell us.
